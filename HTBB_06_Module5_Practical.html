Practical Safeguarding in Ministry

Serving Safely with Children and Vulnerable Adults

Safeguarding is not only about responding to abuse when it occurs. It is also about creating ministry environments that reduce the risk of harm and promote safety for everyone.

The following guidelines help ensure that ministry with children and vulnerable adults is conducted in ways that are safe, transparent, and accountable.

Part A

Practical Safeguarding with Children

Key principles and practices for ministry with children.

Key Principles

Children are a particularly vulnerable group because they depend on adults for care, guidance, and protection. As ministry leaders and volunteers, we are responsible for ensuring that church environments are safe, nurturing, and respectful.

When serving children:

  • Always prioritise the child's safety and wellbeing. Children's physical, emotional, and spiritual safety must always come before ministry convenience.
  • Maintain transparency and accountability. Ministry activities should always take place in ways that are visible and accountable to other leaders and parents.
  • Avoid situations where you are alone with a child. This protects children from harm and protects volunteers from misunderstanding or false accusations.
  • Respect boundaries in communication and physical contact. Healthy boundaries help ensure relationships remain appropriate and safe.
1Never Be Alone With a Child

There should always be at least two approved volunteers present when children are in a ministry space. This ensures that children are protected, volunteers are accountable, and situations are less likely to be misunderstood.

If an unexpected situation occurs where you are temporarily alone with a child:

  • Ensure the door remains open
  • Maintain a clear line of sight from others
  • Move to a public or visible space where possible
Important: Volunteers should never intentionally arrange to meet a child alone.
2Open and Visible Environments

Children's ministry should always take place in open and observable environments. Classrooms and ministry spaces should have see-through windows where possible, or have doors kept open during ministry time.

This allows parents, ministry leaders, and other volunteers to observe activities and helps create a culture of openness and accountability. Open environments protect both children and volunteers.
3Appropriate Physical Contact

Physical contact should always be appropriate, brief, and clearly non-intrusive.

Acceptable forms of contact:

  • High fives
  • Handshakes
  • Fist bumps
  • Brief side hugs initiated by the child

Avoid:

  • Lengthy or full hugs
  • Sitting children on your lap
  • Tickling
  • Piggyback rides
  • Holding children unnecessarily
  • Assisting children in the restroom
If a younger child needs assistance, another volunteer should be present and parents should be informed.
4Communication Boundaries

Digital communication and social media can present safeguarding risks if not handled carefully.

Volunteers must not:

  • Message children privately
  • Interact one-on-one with children on social media
  • Exchange personal contact details with children
  • Share personal photos, videos, or content with children

Communication with children should always take place through official ministry channels, in group chats where other leaders are present, and with parental awareness where appropriate.

5Ministry Concern Report

If a child becomes injured, unwell, or involved in an incident during ministry, volunteers should inform the ministry leader or service leader immediately, complete a Ministry Concern Report, and ensure the child's parents or guardians are informed.

Ministry Concern Report

Used for everyday situations such as injuries, minor accidents, or a child feeling unwell during ministry.

Safeguarding Incident Report

Used when there are concerns about a child or vulnerable person's safety or wellbeing, including possible harm, abuse, or neglect.
