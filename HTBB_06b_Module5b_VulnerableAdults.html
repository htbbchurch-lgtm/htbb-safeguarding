Safeguarding Vulnerable Adults

Who Are Vulnerable Adults?

A vulnerable adult, or an adult with safeguarding needs, refers to someone who may need additional protection because they may be less able to safeguard themselves from harm, abuse, or exploitation.

This may include individuals with:

  • Learning disabilities
  • Mental health challenges
  • Dementia or cognitive decline
  • Substance misuse issues
  • Physical disabilities or long-term illness
  • Reduced capacity to make safe decisions

Some individuals may only be vulnerable in certain situations or seasons of life, such as during grief, financial hardship, illness, or social isolation.

Safeguarding vulnerable adults involves respecting their dignity and independence, while also taking appropriate steps to protect them from harm or exploitation.

The goal is not to remove a person's independence, but to ensure they are supported, safe, and able to participate meaningfully in church life.
Key Principles in Safeguarding Vulnerable Adults

When engaging with vulnerable adults in ministry, these principles guide how we serve with care, wisdom, and responsibility:

1️⃣ Empowerment

Where possible, individuals should be supported to make their own decisions and express their wishes.

This means:

  • Listening carefully to their voice
  • Respecting their choices
  • Avoiding taking over decisions unnecessarily

Safeguarding is not about control, but about ensuring safety while preserving dignity and autonomy.

2️⃣ Prevention

It is always better to prevent harm before it happens.

This includes:

  • Maintaining clear and healthy ministry boundaries
  • Avoiding situations where unhealthy dependency may develop
  • Ensuring transparency in pastoral relationships

Simple, consistent practices help reduce risk and protect both the individual and the volunteer.

3️⃣ Proportionality

Safeguarding responses should be appropriate to the level of risk involved.

Not every concern requires the same level of action. However:

  • Any concern involving harm, abuse, or exploitation must always be reported
  • Volunteers should never attempt to manage serious concerns on their own

When in doubt, always seek guidance from a Safeguarding Officer or ministry leader.

4️⃣ Protection

Extra care should be given to individuals who may be more vulnerable to harm or exploitation.

This may include:

  • Those with limited support networks
  • Those who struggle to advocate for themselves
  • Those experiencing crisis, instability, or emotional distress

Safeguarding means ensuring that people are not left in situations where they could be harmed, even unintentionally.

5️⃣ Partnership

Safeguarding is never meant to be done alone.

When supporting vulnerable adults, we should work together with:

  • Ministry leaders
  • The safeguarding team
  • Families or carers (where appropriate)
  • Relevant professionals or organisations when needed

Shared awareness and accountability help provide safer, healthier care — for both the individual and the volunteer.

Practical Safeguarding in Ministry

Serving vulnerable adults often involves pastoral care, practical support, and friendship. These are important expressions of Christian love, but they must always be carried out within healthy safeguarding boundaries.

1Avoid Dependency Relationships

Pastoral care should never rely on a single individual.

When one person becomes the only source of support, it can lead to:

  • Unhealthy emotional dependence
  • Blurred boundaries
  • Increased safeguarding risks

Instead, support should involve a wider network, such as:

  • Ministry teams or connect group leaders
  • Safeguarding officers
  • Pastoral staff
  • Appropriate external professionals where necessary
Where possible, ensure that others are aware of and involved in the care being provided. This creates accountability and ensures more sustainable, healthy support.
2Maintain Healthy Boundaries

Clear and consistent boundaries protect both vulnerable individuals and those serving in ministry.

Volunteers should avoid:

  • Providing personal financial support or loans
  • Giving or receiving gifts of significant value
  • Becoming someone's sole source of emotional support
  • Offering regular one-to-one counselling without oversight
  • Meeting vulnerable adults in isolated or private settings without informing ministry leaders
Important: If someone requires significant emotional, financial, or practical support, this should be addressed through appropriate church structures or external services — not handled alone.
3Be Aware of Individuals Who May Pose a Risk

Churches are open and welcoming communities, but there may be situations where individuals present safeguarding risks.

In rare cases, someone attending church may:

  • Have a known history of abuse
  • Be under investigation
  • Have previously harmed others
  • Display behaviour that raises concern

If you become aware of such situations:

➡️ Do not attempt to handle it alone

➡️ Inform a Safeguarding Officer, ministry leader, or HTBB staff member immediately

The safeguarding team will assess the situation and determine appropriate next steps. In some cases, specific measures may be put in place to ensure the safety of vulnerable individuals.
